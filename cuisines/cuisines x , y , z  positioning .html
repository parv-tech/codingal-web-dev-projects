Naan

Naan is a popular type of leavened flatbread that
originates from South Asia, particularly India, though
it has roots in Persian cuisine. Traditionally baked in a
tandoor (a cylindrical clay oven), naan is known for its
soft, pillowy texture with slightly crispy, charred
edges. It's typically made from white flour, yeast,
salt, and yogurt or milk, which gives it a distinct tang
and tenderness. Naan is often served hot and brushed
with ghee or butter, and it may be topped with garlic,
herbs, or stuffed with ingredients like cheese or
minced meat. While naan is a staple in Indian cuisines,
it has become popular globally, commonly served with
curries, lentils, and grilled meats .

Palak paneer

Palak paneer is a classic North Indian vegetarian dish made from pureed spinach (palak) and paneer, a fresh, non-melting Indian cheese. The dish features a vibrant green spinach gravy flavored with aromatic spices such as cumin, garlic, ginger, and garam masala. Paneer cubes are usually lightly fried or added directly into the spinach sauce, absorbing the rich flavors. Palak paneer is both nutritious and flavorful, offering a good source of protein, iron, and calcium. It is commonly enjoyed with naan, roti, or rice, and is a staple in Indian households and restaurants alike. Its mild, creamy taste makes it accessible even to those new to Indian cuisine.

Navrattan korma

Navrattan Korma is a rich and flavorful Indian curry known for its creamy texture and colorful assortment of ingredients. The name Navrattan means "nine gems," referring to the traditional inclusion of nine different vegetables, fruits, or nuts in the dish. Typically made with a mix of vegetables like carrots, peas, potatoes, and bell peppers, it also includes sweet elements such as pineapple or raisins, along with cashews or almonds for crunch. The base is a mildly spiced, creamy sauce made from yogurt, cream, or coconut milk, often thickened with ground nuts. Navrattan Korma is a Mughlai-inspired dish, showcasing the fusion of savory and sweet flavors. It is usually served with naan or basmati rice and is especially popular at festive or celebratory meals.

Pakhala

Pakhala is a traditional fermented rice dish from the eastern Indian state of Odisha, and it's especially popular in neighboring regions like West Bengal, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, and Assam. Made by soaking cooked rice in water, often overnight, pakhala is typically consumed cold and may be seasoned with salt and sometimes curd (yogurt), green chilies, or mustard seeds. The soaking process allows slight fermentation, which imparts a tangy flavor and aids in digestion. It is often served with simple side dishes like fried or mashed vegetables, green chilies, or fish fry. Pakhala is especially popular during the hot summer months as it cools the body and helps prevent heatstroke. In Odisha, March 20 is even celebrated as Pakhala Dibasa (Pakhala Day), honoring this humble, nourishing dish.

Gulab Jamun

Gulab Jamun is one of the most beloved and iconic desserts in Indian cuisine. These soft, round sweets are made from a dough of milk solids, usually khoya (reduced milk), mixed with a small amount of flour and sometimes paneer. The dough is shaped into small balls, deep-fried until golden brown, and then soaked in a fragrant sugar syrup flavored with cardamom, rose water, or saffron. The name "Gulab Jamun" comes from Persian words: gulab (rose) referring to the rose-scented syrup, and jamun, a fruit similar in size and color to the sweet. Gulab Jamun is often served warm or at room temperature and is a staple at festivals, weddings, and celebratory meals across South Asia. Its rich, melt-in-the-mouth texture and aromatic sweetness make it a favorite treat for people of all ages.
